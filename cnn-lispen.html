Predictive Maintenance & Optimal Decision Threshold

04 — CNN / LISPEN ENSAM Lille · PJT1A · 2025–2026 Supervisor: Prof. Stephan · ENSAM Lille

Context & Motivation

This is a PJT1A group project (second semester, ENSAM Lille) starting end of April 2026. The base assignment: given vibration data from industrial bearings, build and train a CNN classifier to distinguish fault types. In parallel, I am developing a personal extension: using the classifier's probability outputs as inputs to a formal maintenance optimization problem.

The OR extension idea was presented to — and validated by — Prof. Stephan, who also suggested connecting it with ongoing work at the LISPEN lab (Laboratoire d'Ingénierie des Systèmes Physiques et Numériques). Two preliminary meetings have already taken place with LISPEN researchers, exploring their work on synthetic fault data generators and neural network-based diagnostics. The collaboration is an ongoing discussion.

The bridge: Most ML-for-maintenance work stops at classification accuracy. This project asks: given a classifier with known uncertainty, what decision threshold minimizes expected total cost? That is an optimization problem, not a modeling problem.

Part 1 — CNN Fault Classifier (base project)

Dataset: CWRU Bearing Data

Case Western Reserve University (CWRU) bearing dataset — the standard benchmark for machinery fault detection. Vibration signals from drive-end bearings under four conditions:

ClassDescriptionSignal characteristic
NormalHealthy bearingLow-amplitude broadband noise
Inner race faultDefect on inner ringPeriodic impulses at BPFI frequency
Outer race faultDefect on outer ringPeriodic impulses at BPFO frequency
Ball faultRolling element defectImpulses at BSF frequency

Preprocessing pipeline

Raw time-series → Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) → Mel-frequency spectrogram → CNN input (2D image). The mel scale emphasizes lower frequencies where mechanical fault signatures typically concentrate.

Architecture

LayerConfiguration
Conv2D × 332→64→128 filters, 3×3 kernels, ReLU, BatchNorm
MaxPooling × 32×2
GlobalAveragePoolingReduces spatial dimensions
Dense → Softmax4-class output: [P(normal), P(inner), P(outer), P(ball)]

The softmax output produces a probability vector $\mathbf{p} = [P(\text{normal}), P(\text{inner}), P(\text{outer}), P(\text{ball})]$. For the OR extension, the four classes are aggregated into 3 operational states: normal (class 0 unchanged), degraded (minor fault — small-defect variants of inner/outer), and faulty (severe fault — large-defect variants and ball fault), with the fault probability used in the threshold decision defined as $P(\text{faulty}) = P(\text{inner}) + P(\text{outer}) + P(\text{ball})$. This re-labeling is applied at inference time; the CNN is trained on the original 4-class labels.

Part 2 — OR Extension: Optimal Threshold (personal initiative)

Problem statement

Given the classifier output $\mathbf{p}$, we must decide: intervene for preventive maintenance now, or continue running. The threshold $\theta$ governs this decision: intervene if $P(\text{faulty}) \geq \theta$.

The economic problem: setting $\theta$ too low triggers costly unnecessary interventions (false alarms); too high risks undetected failures (expensive breakdowns). Find $\theta^*$ that minimizes expected total cost.

Formulation

$$\min_{\theta \in [0,1]} \; C(\theta) = c_{\text{prev}} \cdot P(\text{FP}(\theta)) + c_{\text{failure}} \cdot P(\text{FN}(\theta))$$

where:

$$\begin{aligned} &P(\text{FP}(\theta)) = P\!\left(\hat{y}=\text{fault} \mid y=\text{normal}\right) \quad \text{(unnecessary intervention)} \\[6pt] &P(\text{FN}(\theta)) = P\!\left(\hat{y}=\text{normal} \mid y=\text{fault}\right) \quad \text{(missed breakdown)} \end{aligned}$$

Availability constraint

$$\text{subject to:} \quad A(\theta) = 1 - \frac{\mathbb{E}[\text{downtime}(\theta)]}{\mathbb{E}[\text{total time}]} \geq 0.95$$

Optimality conditions

In the population sense, $C(\theta)$ is differentiable in $\theta$ (FP and FN rates are CDFs of the classifier score, continuous under mild distributional assumptions). On the empirical validation set, a kernel-smoothed estimate of the cost curve is used to enable gradient descent. Writing the availability constraint as $g(\theta) = 0.95 - A(\theta) \leq 0$, the KKT conditions at the optimum give:

$$\begin{aligned} &\frac{dC}{d\theta} - \lambda \frac{dA}{d\theta} = 0 \\[6pt] &\lambda \geq 0, \quad \lambda \cdot (A(\theta^*) - 0.95) = 0 \end{aligned}$$

If the availability constraint is not binding ($A(\theta^*) > 0.95$), the unconstrained minimum is optimal: $dC/d\theta = 0$, which yields the classical cost-weighted ROC threshold. When the constraint binds ($\lambda > 0$), $\theta^*$ is pushed toward more conservative intervention (lower threshold) — the cost of missed breakdowns outweighs the gain from reducing false alarms.

Connection to TIPE: This is the same mathematical structure — constrained nonlinear optimization, KKT conditions at the boundary — applied to a fundamentally different physical domain. The skeleton is identical.

Resolution

The cost curve $C(\theta)$ is estimated on the validation set via kernel smoothing (Gaussian kernel, bandwidth selected by cross-validation) to recover a differentiable approximation. The availability constraint is handled with a log-barrier penalty:

$$\min_{\theta} \; C(\theta) - \epsilon \log\!\left(A(\theta) - 0.95\right)$$

As $\epsilon \to 0^+$, the barrier solution converges to the constrained optimum. In practice, $\epsilon$ is decreased geometrically (outer loop) while an inner gradient descent minimizes the penalized objective at each step. Implemented in Python / scipy (L-BFGS-B on the smoothed + penalized curve).

Honest Limitations

  • CWRU is a well-studied benchmark — the CNN result is not novel. The contribution is the OR extension and the cost-optimal threshold formulation.
  • Cost parameters $c_{\text{prev}}$ and $c_{\text{failure}}$ are synthetic (assumed ratio). Real deployment requires client-specific cost estimation.
  • The availability model assumes stationary failure rates — in practice, degradation is non-stationary (Weibull processes), which would require a dynamic programming formulation.
  • LISPEN research contribution is at an early stage — no published results, no formal authorship yet.

Forward Look

The natural extension of this project is a Markov Decision Process formulation of the maintenance problem: state = (equipment condition estimate, time since last maintenance), action = {inspect, maintain, run}, reward = negative cost, with stochastic transitions governed by the failure model. This would connect directly to graduate coursework in stochastic optimization and dynamic programming.
