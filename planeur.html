Glider Wing — Aerodynamic Study & CFD Simulation

07 — Glider Wing ENSAM Lille · 2025–2026 Supervised academic project · Duo

Context

A supervised academic project at ENSAM Lille, carried out in a duo under professor guidance. The objective: study the aerodynamic behavior of a glider wing through analytical sizing and numerical simulation using computational fluid dynamics (CFD).

The project was structured across three successive deliverables, progressing from theoretical sizing to mesh generation and full CFD simulation. No physical fabrication — the contribution is entirely computational and analytical.

Phase 1 — Wing Sizing

The first phase establishes the analytical baseline: sizing the wing geometry to meet lift and structural requirements for a given flight envelope.

Lift model

Using the thin airfoil theory and the lifting line model (Prandtl), the lift coefficient $C_L$ is related to the angle of attack $\alpha$ and the wing geometry (aspect ratio $\mathcal{A}$, span $b$, chord $c$):

$$C_L = \frac{2\pi \alpha}{1 + \frac{2}{\mathcal{A}}} \qquad \text{where} \quad \mathcal{A} = \frac{b^2}{S}$$

The required lift force $L = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 S C_L$ sets the wing surface $S$ for a given flight speed $V$ and aircraft mass. Total drag is decomposed as:

$$C_D = C_{D_0} + \frac{C_L^2}{\pi \mathcal{A} \, e}$$

where $C_{D_0}$ is the zero-lift (profile) drag coefficient and $e \leq 1$ is the Oswald span efficiency factor ($e = 1$ for an elliptical lift distribution). The sizing iterates over aspect ratio $\mathcal{A}$ to maximize the glide ratio $C_L / C_D$ subject to structural constraints on the spar bending moment.

Phase 2 — Mesh Generation (Star-CCM+)

The second phase moves from analytical to numerical: generating the computational mesh for CFD simulation in Star-CCM+ (Siemens). The mesh defines the spatial discretization of the flow domain around the wing profile.

Key meshing decisions: boundary layer refinement near the wing surface (to capture viscous effects), wake region refinement downstream, and far-field domain sizing to minimize boundary condition artifacts. The mesh quality — characterized by orthogonality, skewness, and aspect ratio of cells — directly conditions the accuracy of the CFD solution.

Phase 3 — CFD Simulation & Python Post-Processing

The final phase runs the full CFD simulation and post-processes results in Python. The simulation solves the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations on the generated mesh, with a $k$-$\omega$ SST turbulence model adapted for external aerodynamics.

Key outputs

$C_L$
Lift coefficient — simulated vs. analytical
$C_D$
Drag coefficient — pressure + viscous components
$C_L/C_D$
Aerodynamic efficiency at design point

Python post-processing extracts pressure distributions, velocity fields, and integral aerodynamic coefficients from Star-CCM+ output files. The results are compared against the Phase 1 analytical predictions to quantify the validity of the thin airfoil assumption.

Honest Limitations

  • No physical fabrication or experimental validation — simulation only
  • RANS turbulence model introduces modeling error in separated flow regimes (high angle of attack)
  • Geometry limited to 2D airfoil profile + finite span; no 3D wing-body interaction
  • Academic constraints on simulation time limited mesh refinement to moderate resolution
